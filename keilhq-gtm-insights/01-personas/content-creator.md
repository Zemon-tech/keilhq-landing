# Content Creator View of KeilHQ

**First reaction:** "There are real stories here. Not manufactured ones. The product actually does something unusual — it forces a definition of done before work begins, and it records meetings and turns them into tasks before you leave the call. Those are moments people relate to."

**Stories that are real and can be told honestly:**

1. **The "we built the wrong thing" story** — The Clarity Engine exists because of a recognizable problem: someone creates a task called "fix the thing," a developer spends three days building the wrong version, and the demo call is uncomfortable. KeilHQ structurally prevents this at the database level — you can't create a task without objectives and success criteria. *This is confirmed code behavior, not a UX aspiration.*

2. **The "meeting decision that vanished" story** — A client call ends. Decisions were made. Nobody remembers which decisions became tasks. KeilHQ records the meeting, Sarvam AI or ElevenLabs transcribes it with speaker labels (who said what), and there's a button to turn decisions into tasks before the call ends. *All of this is real, confirmed from the meeting routes and transcription provider code.*

3. **The "our team works in three languages" story** — The Sarvam AI integration supports 23 Indian languages with auto language detection. A multilingual team that's been forced to use English-only transcription tools (Otter.ai, Fireflies) and getting inaccurate transcripts has a real story to tell here. *Confirmed in code with explicit comment: "works well for Indian languages + English."*

4. **The "I asked the AI what's blocking us and it actually knew" story** — Unlike Notion AI or a generic ChatGPT prompt, KeilHQ's AI has specialist agents with direct access to your tasks, calendar, docs, and chat. You can ask "what's overdue in my team right now?" and get real task data back. *Confirmed from the 4-agent architecture in code.* This is genuinely different from every "AI" button bolted onto a task tool.

5. **The "I cancelled four tools in my first month" story** — This is a real outcome that early users can describe after actually using the product. Content creators should find and interview real users to get this story, not invent it.

**What to avoid:**

- Do not write a story that cites "7,000+ teams" — this number is not verified.
- Do not write a story that names specific customers or testimonial givers until they are confirmed real people who have consented.
- Do not write "Google Gemini-powered" — the AI backend is OpenRouter by default.
- Do not write that the product has a mobile app — it explicitly blocks mobile viewports.

**Content formats that work for this product:**

- **Screen recording, 60–90 seconds**: AI answering "what's blocking my team?" in a real workspace. No narration needed — the specificity of the answer (real task names, real dates) makes the point.
- **Before/after thread (Twitter/X or LinkedIn)**: "Before: we used 5 tools. After: we use 1. Here's what actually happened." Written by a real user, not branded content.
- **Founder's honest essay**: "Here's why we built the Clarity Engine" — the honest story of what problem it solves, told from a founding perspective. Authenticity here is more valuable than polish.
- **The Sarvam AI angle for India tech community**: "We built meeting transcription that works in Tamil, Hindi, Telugu, and English in the same call. Here's why English-first tools fail multilingual teams." This is a technically interesting story with underserved audience.

**Fastest objection:** "This sounds like a press release." — Fix: every piece of content should start with a problem someone experienced, not a product announcement. The product is the answer to a problem that exists in the story, not the subject of the story.
